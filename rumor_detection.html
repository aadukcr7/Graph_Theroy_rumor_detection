The Problem

Rumor vs. Organic Propagation

The structure of how information spreads is a powerful signal — more reliable than content analysis alone.

Rumor Graph

  • Star-like structure with high degree
  • Small diameter — rapid spread
  • Many users repost directly from source
  • Simultaneous, burst-like propagation

Organic Graph

  • Chain-like structure with low degree
  • Larger diameter — gradual spread
  • Sequential sharing through networks
  • Slow, organic propagation pattern

How It Works

The Technical Pipeline

Social Media Post→ Propagation Tree→ Temporal Weighting→ GCN→ Feature Aggregation→ Classification

⏱ Temporal Edge Weighting

Each edge in the propagation tree is weighted by how quickly the repost happened. Faster reposts indicate stronger influence and are characteristic of rumor spreading.

w(i, j) = 1 / log(1 + Δt)
where Δt = time delay in minutes between posts

Example: repost within 1 minute → weight ≈ 1.0  |  repost after 1 day → weight ≈ 0.3

🕸 Propagation Tree Structure

Every social media post becomes a node. Every retweet or reply creates an edge. Together, these nodes and edges reveal the journey of information through the network.

Node features include: follower count, account age, text sentiment (optional), time since source post.

Edge features include: temporal weight and interaction type (retweet vs. reply).

🧠 Graph Convolutional Network

The GCN learns structural signatures directly from the graph topology. It aggregates neighbor information through multiple convolution layers, capturing both local and global propagation patterns.

The model distinguishes star-like rumor structures (high degree, small diameter) from chain-like organic structures (low degree, large diameter).

Limitations & Future Work

Current Challenges

⚠ Propagation Data Dependency

The model requires interaction history and diffusion patterns to make predictions — it cannot evaluate isolated posts.

⚠ Early Detection Challenge

Very early-stage rumors may not have enough propagation data to form a meaningful graph for analysis.

⚠ Computational Complexity

Large social graphs require significant processing power and memory, limiting real-time deployment at scale.

⚠ Data Availability

Access to social media APIs and complete propagation information may be restricted or limited by platform policies.
